Journal Overview

This journal documents key learnings, reflections, and experiences gained during the Enterprise IT Project Management module.

Unit 1 – Introduction to Cloud Fundamentals and Services

Unit 1 developed a foundational understanding of cloud computing, focusing on its evolution, core service models, and impact on modern IT. Learning about IaaS, PaaS, and SaaS clarified how responsibility is distributed between providers and customers, helping to evaluate which model best suits organisational needs. This unit also encouraged reflection on the balance between scalability, cost efficiency, and security, which often determine successful adoption. The formative discussion comparing AWS and Google Cloud deepened this insight by contrasting flexibility, pricing, and use cases. It became clear that provider selection depends less on brand and more on aligning capabilities with strategic goals and workload demands.

Unit 2 – Understanding Cloud Architecture and Implementing Frameworks

Unit 2 strengthened architectural thinking by linking strategic frameworks to practical implementation. Analysing TOGAF illustrated how governance and enterprise alignment form the backbone of effective cloud design, while the Software Development Life Cycle (SDLC) emphasised disciplined progression through planning, testing, and deployment. Exploring Infrastructure as Code (IaC) demonstrated how automation translates architecture into repeatable, auditable configurations. The formative discussion reviewing Anggraini, Binariswanto, and Legowo (2019) highlighted how ROCCA complements TOGAF in structured environments, such as the government. Reflecting on this integration underscores that successful architecture combines strategic governance with flexible execution, ensuring both compliance and adaptability in cloud projects..

Unit 3 – Cloud Design Tools

Unit 3 introduced Artificial Intelligence (AI) as a transformative force in cloud management. Through learning about AI-driven automation, optimisation, and predictive analytics, the importance of using data to manage capacity, cost, and performance proactively became evident. AI’s potential to strengthen cloud security and monitoring encouraged reflection on how automation supports resilience while reducing human error. The unit connected theory to practical planning for future workloads that can self-adjust to demand. The formative discussion evaluated cloud design tools, concluding that Microsoft Visio offers advantages through strong Microsoft 365 integration, collaborative architecture modelling, and governance support, reinforcing how tool selection shapes clarity and communication in system design.

Unit 4 – Cloud Native Technology (Part 1)

Unit 4 moved from theory to practical application through scripting and automation. Working with Bash and Python highlighted how scripting enforces consistency, reduces manual intervention, and enables scalable management across cloud resources. Exploring OpenStack demonstrated how open-source platforms provide flexibility for custom deployment and hybrid integration, while GUI based tools offer user-friendly control for routine tasks. Reflection on this experience revealed that automation is not just a technical convenience, it is essential for operational efficiency and compliance. The formative discussion involved designing and executing a Bash script to automate provisioning on OpenStack, reinforcing the value of code-driven management and repeatable infrastructure builds.

Unit 5 – Cloud Native Technology (Part 2)

Unit 5 deepened understanding of cloud-native development through the 12-Factor App methodology and container orchestration with Kubernetes. Applying these principles showed how loosely coupled, stateless applications simplify scaling and deployment. Setting up a local Kubernetes cluster and managing containers revealed both the power and complexity of orchestration, especially the need for observability and robust configuration management. Reflection on this unit clarified that true scalability depends on aligning development practices with infrastructure automation. The formative discussion required deploying a 12-Factor App using Docker and Kubernetes, which provided hands-on insight into packaging, deploying, and maintaining applications that can adapt to demand without compromising reliability.

Unit 6 – Hybrid Cloud, Fog, and Edge Computing

Unit 6 broadened understanding by exploring hybrid, fog, and edge computing as extensions of cloud strategy. Hybrid models were recognised as essential for balancing compliance, performance, and cost, especially in sectors handling sensitive data. Fog and edge technologies illustrated how moving computation closer to data sources improves latency and real-time responsiveness. Reflection on the risks of vendor lock-in and multi-cloud complexity highlighted the importance of open standards and portability in long-term planning. The unit concluded with a team assignment evaluating Microsoft Azure Resource Manager. This collaboration strengthened critical understanding of how governance frameworks underpin hybrid architectures and how real-world implementations achieve both control and agility.

Unit 7 – Cloud Security and Compliance

Unit 7 strengthened understanding of security and compliance as core components of cloud governance rather than afterthoughts. The learning explored the shared responsibility model, encryption, identity management, and vulnerability assessment techniques aligned with ISO/IEC 27001 and GDPR. Evaluating these standards encouraged reflection on how security controls must be embedded throughout system design to ensure confidentiality, integrity, and availability. A key insight was recognising that compliance is continuously maintained through monitoring, patching, and audit readiness. The formative discussion involved performing a security audit on an OpenStack application using OpenVAS, identifying vulnerabilities, recommending mitigations, and mapping results to ISO/IEC 27001 controls, reinforcing the value of structured, standards-based assurance.

Unit 8 – Disaster Recovery and Business Continuity

Unit 8 focused on the design and implementation of disaster recovery (DR) and business continuity strategies to maintain service availability during outages or system failures. The learning emphasised the importance of defining Recovery Time Objectives (RTO) and Recovery Point Objectives (RPO) to balance performance, resilience, and cost. Exploring cloud-based tools such as Azure Site Recovery and AWS Elastic Disaster Recovery provided perspective on automated replication and failover solutions. The practical activity on OpenStack involved simulating a failure scenario and performing recovery using open-source tools like Bacula and Duplicity. The exercise demonstrated how planning, automation, and regular testing underpin a reliable recovery strategy and strengthen organisational resilience.

Unit 9 – Cloud Migration and Integration Strategies

Unit 9 explored the complexities of migrating legacy systems and integrating them into cloud environments. The learning focused on ensuring minimal downtime, maintaining data integrity, and achieving interoperability between on-premises and cloud platforms. Tools such as AWS Database Migration Service (DMS) and Azure Migration Tools were reviewed to understand automation and validation processes that reduce human error. Key challenges identified included dependency mapping, version compatibility, and post-migration performance tuning. The formative activity involved migrating a MySQL database from a local environment to OpenStack using Percona XtraBackup, demonstrating data consistency and near-zero downtime. This exercise reinforced the importance of structured planning, testing, and rollback procedures in successful cloud migration projects.

Unit 10 – Advanced Cloud Technologies (Serverless Computing)

Unit 10 explored serverless computing as a key advancement in modern cloud architecture, highlighting its ability to improve scalability, efficiency, and cost management by abstracting infrastructure management from developers. The learning focused on platforms such as AWS Lambda, Azure Functions, and OpenFaaS, examining how event-driven execution and automatic scaling support lightweight, modular applications. Reflection centred on how serverless models enable faster development cycles while reducing idle resource costs, making them ideal for intermittent workloads. The formative discussion required developing and deploying a simple function in OpenFaaS, which demonstrated how automation, scale-to-zero capabilities, and integrated monitoring optimise cloud operations through simplicity and responsiveness.

Unit 11 – AI and Cloud Computing

Unit 11 concluded the module by exploring the integration of Artificial Intelligence (AI) within cloud computing and its growing impact on automation, optimisation, and innovation. The learning examined how cloud providers such as AWS, Azure, and Google Cloud embed AI capabilities into their platforms through services like SageMaker, Azure AI, and Google AI to enhance data analytics, automation, and decision-making. Reflection on these developments highlighted how AI transforms cloud infrastructure into adaptive systems that intelligently manage workloads, security, and costs. The final individual assignment involved producing a cloud strategy report that integrated AI, automation, hybrid cloud, and Infrastructure as Code. This assessment reinforced how emerging technologies can drive scalability, compliance, and long-term resilience in cloud design.

Unit 12 – Future Trends in Cloud Computing

Unit 12 explored emerging technologies shaping the future of cloud computing, focusing on Artificial Intelligence (AI), Blockchain, and Quantum Computing. AI was identified as a key driver of automation and intelligent optimisation, Blockchain as a tool for transparency and security, and Quantum Computing as a potential breakthrough for processing power and encryption. Reflection on these trends highlighted the need for adaptability and continuous learning to stay relevant in a rapidly evolving field. The final e-Portfolio assignment consolidated learning from all twelve units through reflective analysis, skills evaluation, industry application, and future learning goals. It demonstrated how cloud computing knowledge, from architecture and automation to AI integration, can be applied critically and effectively in real-world contexts.
