Journal Overview

This journal documents key learnings, reflections, and experiences gained during the Enterprise IT Project Management module.

Unit 1: Introduction to ETI

In Week 1, The fundamentals of Enterprise IT (EIT) were thoroughly examined, differentiating it from related concepts such as Business Information Systems and Industry 4.0/5.0. Key EIT concepts, including its historical evolution and the EIT knowledge tree, were explored. The interactions between EIT and standards such as TOGAF and ITIL, as well as bodies of knowledge (BOKs) like PMBOK and SEBOK, were also analysed. By the end of the week, a comprehensive understanding of EIT's definition and applications was developed, relevant BOKs for EIT practitioners were identified, and transformative EIT components that have influenced business practices were reviewed.

Unit 2: EIT vs. Shadow IT

In Week 2, The contrast between Enterprise IT (EIT) and Shadow IT was examined. EIT refers to officially sanctioned technologies that adhere to formal processes, whereas Shadow IT involves unsanctioned, often ad hoc solutions that can foster innovation but also present risks. The sources of Shadow IT, reasons for its adoption, and its potential benefits and drawbacks were discussed. Insights from this week emphasised the need to balance the creative potential of Shadow IT with the management of associated risks. Understanding these dynamics is essential for businesses to effectively evaluate and integrate Shadow IT while maintaining security and compliance.

Unit 3: EIT and Automation

In Week 3, The relationship between Enterprise IT (EIT) and automation was explored, tracing automation's historical origins and its evolution alongside computer systems. The examination focused on how automation, enabled by programming languages, enhances business processes and drives digital transformation. Key topics included the application of various programming languages for automation, with particular attention to BASIC in office environments, and the alignment of automation with Industry 4.0 and 5.0. The discussions underscored the strategic use of automated systems to streamline operations, select suitable programming languages for specific tasks, and understand automation's contribution to advancing modern industry practices and sustainability.

Unit 4: A Comparative Study: Practical Automation Exercises using C and Python

In Week 4, Practical programming exercises focused on implementing and analysing a sorting algorithm using Python and C. The activities provided insights into how compiled and interpreted languages influence performance and the role of algorithmic optimisation in execution time. By designing and comparing sorting programs in both languages, the exercise highlighted differences in performance and the underlying reasons for these variations. This exploration emphasised the importance of language selection and code optimisation for enhancing efficiency. Additionally, the week’s activities related to broader trends in Enterprise IT, including the effects of automation and emerging technologies on programming practices.

Unit 5: Business Systems, Outsourcing and the Cloud

In Week 5, The focus was on the evolution of business systems, the dynamics of outsourcing, and the emergence of cloud systems as a transformative force. The discussion encompassed the development of business systems, including the various models and technologies. Outsourcing, along with its variants such as offshoring and nearshoring, was examined, highlighting the benefits and challenges associated with each approach. The week also covered public Cloud Solution Providers (CSPs), exploring the advantages and disadvantages of cloud hosting and computing. Insights gained from this week provided a comprehensive understanding of the impact of these systems and strategies on modern business practices.

Unit 6: A Cost Comparison Between On-premise, Private and Public Cloud Hosting

In Week 6, a comparative analysis of on-premise, private, and public cloud hosting was conducted, focusing on cost differences and technical considerations. The week covered the design and setup of cloud-based versus on-premise systems, including the “lift and shift” migration process. A detailed comparison of hosting costs for both on-premise and cloud solutions was performed. The insights provided a clear understanding of the pros and cons associated with each hosting option, facilitated the calculation of comparative costs, and enabled the recommendation of the most suitable hosting solution for specific systems based on cost-effectiveness and technical requirements.

Unit 7: Software Licensing: Commercial vs. Open Source

In Week 7, The focus was on software licensing, specifically comparing commercial and open-source licenses. Discussions covered the advantages and disadvantages of commercial licensing agreements and various open-source licensing options. The week also addressed future challenges related to digital rights management (DRM). Key insights included when to opt for commercial versus open source licenses, the pros and cons of different licensing approaches, and the implications of emerging technologies on licensing models. Understanding these aspects is crucial for making informed decisions about software licensing that align with business needs and operational goals. The assessment involved comparing programming languages and sorting algorithms, emphasising the practical application of these concepts.

Unit 8: A Case Study: Investigating Business and Technical Innovations of Large Corporations and SMEs

In Week 8, A case study of IBM and Acorn was conducted to explore how these companies employed technical and business innovations to establish themselves in the emerging personal microcomputer market. The analysis contrasted the strategies of a large global corporation and a small UK startup, highlighting their use of innovation and identifying factors that led to business challenges. Key takeaways included recommendations for modern companies based on exemplary business and technical processes observed, as well as identifying critical mistakes to avoid. This examination underscores the importance of strategic innovation and learning from past errors to guide future business practices.

Unit 9: Communications and Digital Transformation

In Week 9, The focus was on digital communications as a driving force behind the adoption of modern digital technologies. The discussion covered the evolution of communication epochs and the impact of Claude Shannon’s 1948 paper, which laid the foundation for digitization and digitalization. Key topics included pervasive computing and its application to the Internet of Things (IoT), with a particular emphasis on IoT technologies and their use in contemporary industrial systems. Insights were gained on how digital communications enhance industrial processes, the role of IoT in Industry, and the application of BPMN for digitalising traditional processes, highlighting critical elements of digital transformation.

Unit 10: The Digital Transformation Process

In Week 10, The focus was on contemporary techniques and approaches for digital transformation, specifically through practical demonstrations of converting manual processes to digital formats. The use of BPMN and UML for representing digitalised processes was discussed, and the application of a popular design package for creating these designs was demonstrated. Reviews of designs aimed to identify missing steps and potential improvements, providing critical insights into refining digitalisation strategies. This week's activities facilitated the creation and critique of digital transformation designs, emphasising best practices and effective approaches to addressing digitalisation challenges.

Unit 11: Future Trends

In Week 11, emerging trends in Enterprise IT, such as AI, security, and Web 3.0, were examined to assess their potential impact over the next five years. The discussions highlighted how these trends, driven by rapid technological and business advancements, are likely to influence the field of Enterprise IT significantly. Key aspects covered included the implications of Web 3.0, advancements in AI, and evolving security concerns. The evaluation focused on the effects these trends could have on EIT systems, with an emphasis on determining which trend might be most influential in shaping the future of Enterprise IT.

Unit 12: The Great Debate

In Week 12, a debate was conducted to evaluate which of the four identified global trends—AI, automation, Web 3.0, and security—will exert the most significant influence on Enterprise IT over the next five years. This final unit involved selecting and justifying the most impactful trend, creating a presentation to advocate for the chosen trend, and participating in a group vote to determine the most influential trend. The session emphasised the importance of reflecting on the potential industry and workplace impacts of these trends. The individual assignment required developing a PowerPoint presentation and a recorded reflection to critically assess the future implications of the selected trend on industry practices.
